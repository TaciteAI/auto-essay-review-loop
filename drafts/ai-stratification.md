The cleanest version of "AI takes your job" isn't job loss. It's compression of the middle.

AI doesn't just automate tasks — it changes who captures the value of human work. And the answer, increasingly, is whoever owned the model and the distribution channel before the work happened.

Four mechanisms already showing up:

1. Skill premiums collapse for the median, expand for the top. A $40/hr copywriter now competes with a $0.20/1k-token model that's "good enough" for 80% of jobs. A $400/hr creative director who knows what to ask the model for is leveraged 10x. The bottom of the curve gets squeezed; the top extracts a higher share.

2. Capital gates the leverage. Using AI well requires proprietary data, compute budget, and time to learn fast-iterating tooling. All three correlate with existing wealth. A founder with $5M in seed can spend $100k/month on Claude/GPT-5/Gemini and learn what works. A freelancer at $60k/yr cannot.

3. Distribution centralizes. The same algorithms deciding which post you see also decide which AI-generated post you see. Independent creators competing against AI-augmented incumbents are facing opponents who do in 30 minutes what they do in 30 hours, at the same distribution cost.

4. Cognitive work was the escape ladder. For 50 years, "go to college → get a knowledge job → out-earn your parents" worked for tens of millions. AI is most disruptive precisely to the entry-level rungs of that ladder. Junior associate, junior dev, junior analyst — the seats you used to start in are the seats most exposed.

The counter-argument: every general-purpose technology produced this fear, and previous waves (electricity, computing, the internet) created more middle-class jobs than they destroyed. True. The question is whether AI sits in that family, or in a different one — closer to a labor-substituting capital good than a labor-augmenting tool. Early evidence points to the second.

What this means: the cost of staying in place rises. The cost of moving up rises faster. The wealthy aren't getting wealthier because of conspiracy or greed — they're getting wealthier because the new technology disproportionately rewards the inputs they already have.

Treating this as a personal problem ("learn AI to stay relevant") is a mistake. It is a structural problem with structural answers: how labor income is taxed vs capital income, who owns the training data, what the entry-level job market looks like in 2030.

We can ask those questions now. Or we can ask them in five years from a much worse starting position.
