# A Data Hoard Is Not a Data Moat

a16z was right to puncture the lazy claim that proprietary data automatically creates defensibility. Volume is not the moat. In many AI markets, the cost of adding unique data rises while the value of incremental data falls, the opposite of the traditional economies-of-scale story founders like to tell.

That matters because "we have data" has become the weakest sentence in an AI pitch deck. Investors have heard it from every startup crawling public websites, scraping customer documents, or logging user prompts. The relevant question is not whether data exists. It is whether the data is high quality, domain specific, rights-cleared, operationally fresh, and connected to a loop that improves the product as customers use it.

The distinction is now central to AI defensibility. In a market where AI captured [61% of global VC investment in 2025](https://agentmarketcap.ai/blog/2026/04/10/ai-captures-50-percent-global-venture-capital-2025), capital is not scarce for credible companies. Credibility is scarce for data stories that collapse under inspection.

## Why Raw Volume Decays

The old internet platform logic was simple: more users produce more data, more data improves the product, a better product attracts more users. That loop still exists in some cases, but generative AI weakens the naive version. Foundation models are already trained on enormous corpora. Adding a pile of undifferentiated documents rarely changes capability enough to matter.

a16z's essay on [the empty promise of data moats](https://a16z.com/the-empty-promise-of-data-moats/) describes the core trap. Unique data can become more expensive to acquire over time, while each marginal unit contributes less to performance. If the dataset is noisy, stale, legally ambiguous, or only loosely related to the task, it becomes storage cost and compliance risk rather than defensibility.

This is especially true for horizontal products. A general sales assistant that has "lots of sales calls" may still be inferior to a vertical agent trained around one industry's pricing norms, procurement language, renewal triggers, and objection patterns. The latter has less data by volume and more data by meaning.

## The Flywheel Has to Be Operational

A real data moat is not a warehouse. It is a flywheel. The product performs work, observes outcomes, captures corrections, updates the workflow, and becomes more accurate in the specific domain where the customer pays. The loop is valuable because it is tied to action, not because it makes a chart of terabytes go up.

Insignia Business Review's analysis of [proprietary data as a moat](https://review.insignia.vc/2025/03/10/ai-moat/) gets the nuance right: data is weak by itself, but high-quality, domain-relevant datasets with early flywheels can compound. The difference is feedback. A legal intake agent that learns which matters a firm accepts, which facts change routing, and which summaries survive attorney review is building process knowledge. A company archiving random legal documents is not.

The same pattern applies in healthcare, logistics, insurance, and finance. The durable dataset is not merely proprietary. It is generated by use, labeled by business outcomes, and refreshed by the workflow itself. It contains the small, expensive facts that generic models do not know: local exceptions, buyer preferences, compliance boundaries, and failure modes.

## Data Rights Are Part of the Moat

The next mistake is pretending data quality can be separated from data rights. Enterprises will not build critical workflows on datasets that cannot survive procurement review. A defensible AI startup knows what it is allowed to store, how long it can retain it, how corrections are audited, and whether customer-specific learning can improve the global product.

That legal and operational discipline is not bureaucracy. It is part of the product. A data flywheel that cannot be explained to a regulated buyer is not a moat; it is a liability waiting for diligence.

The sharper claim is this: data only compounds when it is scarce, clean, contextual, and connected to repeated work. Everything else is a hoard. In 2026, investors should treat undifferentiated data volume the way they treat undifferentiated model access: useful as an input, fatal as the whole story.
