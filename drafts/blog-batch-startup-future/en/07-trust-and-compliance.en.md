# Trust and Compliance

In regulated markets, the moat is not the model. The model is rented, benchmarked, swapped, and repriced. The moat is the evidence trail around the model: who asked, what data was used, what rule fired, what human approved, what changed afterward, and whether the company can prove all of that six months later under subpoena or regulator review.

Healthcare, finance, and legal services do not reject AI because executives lack imagination. They reject systems that cannot be audited. A demo can tolerate mystery. A hospital, bank, or law firm cannot. The gate is not capability in the abstract. The gate is whether the institution can survive the consequence of delegated action.

## Regulators Define the Product Boundary

The FDA has already made clear that AI in medical software sits inside an evolving oversight regime. Its work on [AI and machine learning software as a medical device](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/software-medical-device-samd/artificial-intelligence-and-machine-learning-software-medical-device) is a reminder that clinical AI cannot be treated like a consumer productivity tool. Claims, updates, data drift, intended use, and post-market monitoring all matter.

Finance has the same structure. FINRA's guidance on [artificial intelligence in broker-dealers](https://www.finra.org/rules-guidance/key-topics/artificial-intelligence) is not a ban on automation. It is a demand that firms preserve supervision, governance, recordkeeping, and accountability. The regulator does not care that the model is impressive. It cares whether the firm can explain and control how the model affects customers.

Legal AI faces a related constraint. Courts and bar associations are not asking whether lawyers can use AI. They are asking whether lawyers remain responsible for accuracy, confidentiality, competence, and disclosure. That turns auditability into product infrastructure.

## The Audit Trail Is the Workflow

A serious regulated AI product should treat the audit trail as a first-class object, not a logging afterthought. Every recommendation should carry source documents, confidence bands, policy checks, versioned prompts, model identifiers, user permissions, and approval state. Every action should have a reversible path or an escalation path.

This is why "AI wrapper" is the wrong insult for the best companies in regulated verticals. The wrapper may be the company. If the wrapper contains policy logic, access control, institution-specific procedure, model evaluation, retention rules, and evidence packaging, then it is not decorative. It is the part the buyer is actually purchasing.

The numbers explain the urgency. Enterprise AI has moved into production, with Menlo Ventures finding [61% of enterprises using generative AI in production](https://menlovc.com/2024-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/). As usage moves from pilots to operating budgets, the informal phase ends. Procurement, compliance, security, and legal teams enter the room. The product either has answers or loses to a slower vendor that does.

## Trust Is Expensive to Copy

Model capability diffuses quickly. Compliance posture does not. It is accumulated through certifications, procurement scars, red-team findings, regulator conversations, customer security reviews, and boring product choices that reduce risk.

A startup selling into hospitals, banks, or law firms should not hide this cost. It should weaponize it. SOC 2 is not enough, but it is a floor. Data residency, role-based access, incident response, retention policy, admin review, and customer-controlled model settings are not enterprise checkboxes. They are the economic moat if the company can turn them into faster deployment and lower perceived risk.

The strongest AI companies in regulated industries will be judged less by frontier benchmark scores than by their ability to make institutions comfortable with delegation. That means proving behavior, not promising intelligence.

## The Regulated Future Is Not Slow

Regulation does not make AI unimportant. It makes the winning architecture different. The valuable company is not the one that shouts "autonomy" first. It is the one that decomposes autonomy into observable, permissioned, reviewable steps.

The next decade of regulated AI will be built by companies that understand the regulator as a product stakeholder. The model may answer the question. The audit trail earns the right to act.
