# When Foundation Models Become Competitors

## The Platform Has Entered the App Layer

For two years, AI startups built on top of foundation model providers with a fragile assumption: the model company would supply intelligence, and the startup would own the workflow. That line is now blurring. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google are no longer merely selling APIs. They are shipping work products, enterprise interfaces, browser agents, document agents, and collaboration tools.

OpenAI's [Operator](https://openai.com/index/introducing-operator/) made this explicit by turning the browser into an execution surface. Anthropic's [Claude for Work](https://www.anthropic.com/claude-for-work) positions Claude inside enterprise collaboration and knowledge workflows. Google's [Gemini for Workspace](https://workspace.google.com/solutions/ai/) brings agents directly into the productivity suite where many knowledge workers already live.

The platform is no longer only infrastructure. It is a competitor with distribution.

## The Old Wrapper Critique Got Real

The phrase "wrapper" was often used lazily. Many software companies are wrappers around deeper infrastructure. The problem is not wrapping. The problem is wrapping without control over distribution, data, workflow, or trust.

When foundation model vendors ship horizontal agents, they absorb the obvious use cases first: summarization, drafting, search, simple browser actions, meeting support, document transformation, inbox triage, and spreadsheet assistance. These are useful features, but they are dangerous startup categories. The model company has better access to the roadmap, cheaper inference economics, and a default relationship with the buyer.

This does not kill the application layer. It kills the application layer that depends on model novelty staying scarce. If the startup's main claim is that it can call a frontier model and arrange the output attractively, the window is closing.

## Startups Are Moving Downstream

The rational startup response is to move closer to the economic event. Not "write a better email," but "recover the invoice." Not "summarize the ticket," but "resolve the case within policy." Not "draft the contract," but "get the supplier agreement approved with auditable changes." The more concrete the workflow, the less attractive it is for a foundation model company to own end to end.

Startups are also moving toward proprietary context. That can mean vertical data, system integrations, historical decisions, human expert review, regulated processes, or implementation knowledge earned through deployments. The point is not to hoard data for its own sake. The point is to build a product that improves because it lives inside a workflow the platform does not fully see.

a16z's [AI application strategy](https://a16z.com/ai-application-strategy/) emphasizes that durable AI applications need more than model access, while the firm's [agent market map](https://a16z.com/ai-agents-market-map/) shows how crowded the horizontal opportunity has become. The strategic lesson is blunt: if the platform can generalize your use case, it eventually will.

## Defensibility Moves to the Boring Parts

The defensible parts of AI startups now look less like magic and more like enterprise software. Permissions. Audit logs. Workflow ownership. Data contracts. Review queues. Implementation playbooks. Domain-specific evaluation. Buyer trust. These are not the things that go viral in a launch video, but they are the things that make a customer renew when the platform announces a similar feature.

Founders should assume their model provider may become a competitor. That assumption is not paranoia. It is a normal platform risk returning under a new name. The right answer is not to avoid foundation models. The right answer is to build where the platform is a component rather than the company.

The startups that survive this phase will not be the ones pretending the giants will stay in their lane. They will be the ones whose lane is too specific, too operational, and too close to customer value for a general model interface to erase.
